## Reflective Summary
**Reflective Model 03 - Network Security**

* **What (happened)**: I describe the experience
* **So what**: I reflect on meaning and learning
* **Now what**: I decide on future actions and improvements

**Introduction**
In this reflection, I apply the 3W model developed by Rolfe et al. (What? So What? Now What?) to critically evaluate my professional learning development in the Network Security module. I reflect on how my theoretical knowledge, technical skills, analytical thinking, and professional awareness developed across Units 1 to 6.

(Rolfe et al., 2001).

## What? – Description of the Experience
In Unit 1, I explored the history of cybersecurity, including the Colossus system, the CIA triad, and the cyber kill chain. This unit introduced key security principles that provided a foundation for understanding modern network security. 
(Kaczmarczyk, 2024).

In Unit 2, I developed a literature-based vulnerability report of Zero Bank, mapping identified weaknesses to CWE with the NVD while adhering to ethical research guidelines, avoiding active exploitation while analyzing Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) and characteristics of Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs).

(MITRE, 2025).


In Unit 3, I conducted vulnerability scans and developed several reports within the fintech industry. Initially, I assumed such reports should prioritize technical depth and precision; however, Later, I was pointed out that effective reporting requires balancing technical notation with precise communication for executive stakeholders. This experience highlighted to me the importance of integrating theoretical frameworks with practical vulnerability analysis.

During Unit 4, I focused on logging and governance mechanisms. I worked on the Log4Shell collaborative assignment. The discussion demonstrated how poorly implemented security mechanisms within software supply chains can introduce significant supply-chain risks. I also gained practical experience using tools within Kali Linux, including enumeration techniques and ethical offensive security testing. I explored defensive controls such as firewalls and proxies for risk mitigation.

In Unit 5, I evaluated a penetration-testing template. I differentiate between vulnerability assessment and structured penetration testing by using the NCSC guidelines.

(NCSC, 2022).

Finally, Unit 6 required synthesizing prior learnings into an executive-level security summary.

## So What? – Evaluation and Analysis
Initially, I considered vulnerability reports as purely technical. However, I realized that they require deeper critical analysis, particularly when communicating findings to executive stakeholders. Unit 3 represented a turning point in my understanding, where a report required significantly greater critical and risk-based analysis, as well as incorporating more governance, risk, and regulatory contexts.

Integrating multiple technical and academic sources proved challenging. Shifting from CVE-level discussions to CWE-level classifications ensured consistency and ethical compliance, strengthening the quality of my analysis.

I also developed a better understanding of how the increasing digitalization of enterprises broadens attack surfaces. I recognized that this expansion also increases regulatory, governance, and compliance responsibilities. Digitalization is not only technical; it is also strategic. This means that it involves careful consideration of ethics and organizational frameworks.

(Doroiman & Sîrghi, 2024).

Furthermore, I became more aware of legal and ethical considerations. I adhered to professional standards by conducting passive audits and black-box scans. I grasped the importance of structured planning, cross-validation of results, and clearly stating limitations to improve the reliability of my analyses.

(NCSC, 2017).

## Now What? – Conclusion and Action Plan
This module has improved my view about the importance of quality analysis integration with governance, ethics, and risk-based thinking. Moving forward I will ensure that my work uses established frameworks and academic rigor, focusing on deeper, clear and methodologically sound analysis with ethically responsible reporting. Additionally, I plan to develop deeper expertise in network security and threat modelling.

(NIST, 2021; NCSC, 2022).

This reflection highlights that resilience in cybersecurity depends not only on identifying vulnerabilities but also on contextualizing them for decision makers highlighting legal, ethical, and organizational impact and consequences.

(Rolfe et al., 2001).
